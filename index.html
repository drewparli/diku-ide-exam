Visualizing Air Pollution in Denmark

In Denmark, air pollution is an ongoing problem in many urban areas, as exposure to high concentrations of airborne particulates can significantly influence the health of citizens. Research as shown that long term exposure can lead to heart and respiratory diseases, as well as strokes and lung cancer. More specifically, researchers have concluded that an increase of only 10 μg/m3 in airborne particulate matter (PM10) can reduce life expectancy by 0.64 years (95% CI: 0.21, 1.07) [1].

Unfortunately, particle concentration data is rather boring, and often people are not sure what terms like nitrogen dioxide or μg/m3 mean. Therefore, the aim of our project is to present this data in a more approachable manner.

To acheive this goal, we translated the quantitative data gathered from an air pollutant monitoring station in Copenhagen, Denmark to more approachable and comparable qualitative data using the Common Air Quality Index (CAQI) developed by the EU's Citeair project. This allows us to speak in general terms about the quality of the air in the area, instead of hoping that a view has enough domain knowledge to make their own interpretations about the data. More information about the index and its scoring is presented in The Common Air Quality Index section. In all, we present three different visualizations of the data, each working at a different time scale: hourly, daily and yearly. The following sections describe the intent of these three visualizations and how they might be used.

Hourly Quality Simulation

The intent of this visualization was to allow the viewer to explore the dataset at the most fine grain level (i.e., hourly). Additionally, instead of simply displaying the values at each interval, we wanted to provide the viewer with a more intuitive representation of the data at each point in time. Therefore, we attempted to "gamify" the dataset by visualizing the concentration of each particle type as a group of bouncing particles, see [6], in a scene that represents the locality where the measurements where taken. We emphasize gamify, since we did not actually create a classic game, but rather a scene that represents this cannon of visualizations. Our hope is that this will help to break down barriers some user's may have to accessing the dataset by presenting it in a context they might consider to be more enjoyable.

Viewers can explore the dataset by either choosing to set the date and time themselves, or by "playing" through the dataset. Again emphasizing that in this context playing means to step through the recorded date points sequentially with respect to time, but also play in the sence of the game scene.

Finally, the quality of the air is show in the barchart at the bottom of the visualization. As the user plays through the dataset, the index value of each particle is shown along with the actual concentration values. Users can click the particle labels to isolate specific particles in the visualization.

The Dataset

In accordance with international conventions such as the Climate Convention and the Geneva Convention on Long-Range Transboundary Air Pollution, the concentrations of specific air particulates are monitored at various intervals across Denmark at specific monitoring stations. Aarhus University's Department of Environmental Science manages the reporting of such data both in Denmark and to the European Union's European Environment Agency (EEA).

Monitoring Stations

The data gathered at each monitoring station is dependent on the location and purpose of the station. In general a single station will monitor one of two different conditions:

  • Roadside conditions
    These stations are placed at the ground level near major streets.
  • Background conditions
    These stations are placed on top of buildings or other structures so as to decrease localized effects like traffic.

H. C. Ørsted Institutet

For this visualization specifically, we used the data gathered at the H. C. Ørsted Institutet roof top monitoring station for 2017. It is station no. 1259, as seen in the image below, and is atop a flat roof, seven story building at the University of Copenhagen. The area is open with trees between the university institutes. The distance to the Jagtvej street station (no. 1257) is around 300 meters and there are several streets with traffic densities above 5,000 vehicles per day within a few kilometres.

Measurements

The monitoring station measures the following: nitrogen dioxide (NO2), ozone (O3), airborne particulates (PM10 and PM25), carbon monoxide (CO) and sulfur dioxide (SO2). Measurements are recorded every half hour, on the hour and at half past the hour. This means we get a very dense dataset for the concentration levels across the entire year, i.e., approximately 17,520 data points for each particle for the entire year. See more information about each particle type in the Particles section.

Gathering the Dataset

Initially, we looked at using the dataset provided on the Open Data Copenhagen website. However, this dataset was rather small, only containing information for a single year. Therefore, we contacted the Department of Environmental Science and requested access to the full dataset, to which they agreed. In fact, we were given API access to all data from 2017-2000, however the consistency of the data, measurement methods and types of particles measured across this period presented many challenges in terms of trying to reconcile the data. Therefore, given the restricted time frame of this project, we chose to focus only on the most recent years worth of data, i.e., 2017.

Preprocessing the Dataset

In order to reduce the load time of the visualization, we preprocessed the data in Python. Essentially, this meant augmenting the existing data measurements with statistical data, e.g., the common air quality index value and daily average. Additionally, in order to reduce the number of elements in the simulation visualization, we created a hash table by date, so that it was easy to update elements based on a date change.

The Common Air Quality Index

In 2004, the Common Information to European Air (Citeair) was started with the goal of developing an efficient means to collect, present and compare air quality data across a multitude of sites in the EU. As a result, the Common Air Quality Index (CAQI) was developed.

As previously mentioned, the monitoring station we selected is a background station, therefore the values on the left-hand side of the chart below determine the index value for each particle type.

Particles
There is a distinction between core polutants and other polutants. Without core polutants, an index value could not be calculated. For city background measurements, the core polutants are NO2, O3 and PM10 while the other polutants are PM2.5, CO and SO2.

Core Polutants

Nitrogen dioxide (NO2)

Nitrogen dioxicde is one of several nitrogen oxides. NO 2 is an intermediate in the industrial synthesis of nitric acid, millions of tons of which are produced each year. At higher temperatures it is a reddish-brown gas that has a characteristic sharp, biting odor and is a prominent air pollutant.

Wikipedia page
Ozone (O3)

Ground-level ozone (O3), unlike other pollutants mentioned, is not emitted directly into the atmosphere, but is a secondary pollutant produced by reaction between nitrogen dioxide (NO2), hydrocarbons and sunlight. Ozone levels are not as high in urban areas (where high levels of NO are emitted from vehicles) as in rural areas. Sunlight provides the energy to initiate ozone formation; consequently, high levels of ozone are generally observed during hot, still sunny, summertime weather.

Wikipedia page
Particulate PM10

Airborne particulate matter varies widely in its physical and chemical composition, source and particle size. PM10 particles (the fraction of particulates in air of very small size (<10 µm)) and PM2.5 particles (<2.5 µm) are of major current concern, as they are small enough to penetrate deep into the lungs and so potentially pose significant health risks. Larger particles meanwhile, are not readily inhaled, and are removed relatively efficiently from the air by sedimentation. The principal source of airborne PM10 and PM2.5 matter in European cities is road traffic emissions, particularly from diesel vehicles. The limit values are very often exceeded in European cities.

Wikipedia page

Other Polutants

Carbon monoxide (CO)

CO is an odourless, tasteless and colourless gas produced by the incomplete burning of materials which contain carbon, including most transport fuels. CO is toxic, acting by reaction with haemoglobin and reducing its capacity for oxygen transport in the blood. Even in busy urban centres, CO concentrations rarely exceed health related standards.

Sulfur dioxide (SO2)

Fossil fuels contain traces of sulphur compounds, and SO2 is produced when they are burnt. The majority of the SO2 emitted to the air is from power generation, and the contribution from transport sources is small (shipping being an exception). Exposure to SO2 can damage health by its action on the bronchial system. Sulphuric acid generated from atmospheric reactions of SO2 is the main constituent of acid rain, and ammonium sulphate particles are the most abundant secondary particles found in air.
